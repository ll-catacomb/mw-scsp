# RA-5: Yuna Wong's Register and the Wargaming-Validation Literature

## Who she is (current affiliations, confirmed via web)

**Dr. Yuna Huh Wong** is currently a **Section Supervisor for Strategy and Policy Analysis within the National Security Analysis Department at the Johns Hopkins University Applied Physics Laboratory (JHU/APL)**. In that role she leads a team developing analytical tools and techniques for problems ranging from multipolar nuclear deterrence to digital wargaming. She is concurrently an **adjunct professor at Georgetown University's Walsh School of Foreign Service**, where she teaches courses on artificial intelligence and national security policy. She is the **founder and president of the Women's Wargaming Network**.

Her career trajectory is the relevant context for our project:

- **Marine Corps Combat Development Command** — early-career operations research analyst, ground floor of the modern USMC wargaming push.
- **RAND Corporation** — long tenure as a policy researcher; rose to **co-director of the RAND Center for Gaming**; led the design team for *Hedgemony*, the boardgame that supported the 2018 National Defense Strategy.
- **Institute for Defense Analyses (IDA)** — research analyst in the Joint Advanced Warfighting Division, leading wargames and tabletop exercises for the Joint Staff and combatant commands.
- **JHU/APL (current)** — running an applied program on incorporating LLMs and other AI into wargaming, including specifically the question of how well LLMs can replicate an adversary.

**Education:** B.S. in Political Science and Economics (MIT); M.A. in Political Science (Columbia); Ph.D. in Policy Analysis (Pardee RAND Graduate School). Her dissertation used agent-based modeling to study noncombatants in U.S. urban operations — note this for our own use of agent-based metaphors in the spec.

She is institutionally a connector across the modern American wargaming community: RAND alumna, ex-IDA, now at APL, board-level at the Women's Wargaming Network, and a regular presence at Connections US.

## Her vocabulary

These are the specific terms and turns of phrase she returns to. We will reuse the register, not the literal phrases.

- **"Hypothesis generation"** vs. **"hypothesis testing"** — she is precise about which one a wargame can do. Games can elicit candidate hypotheses; they cannot confirm them.
- **"Articulating your theory"** — she frames the act of designing a game as forcing the analyst to make their tacit theory explicit and to operationalize its variables.
- **"Causal mechanisms"** — used as a hard limit: there are too many variables in a wargame to draw firm conclusions about causal mechanisms.
- **"External validity"** — used in the technical social-science sense; she points out that wargames generally lack it, which is why their outputs do not translate to predictions.
- **"Validation"** — used skeptically. She is careful that "validate" should mean what it means in M&S / OR — comparison against ground truth — and warns that DoD wargames are often used to "validate" service concepts in a much weaker sense (i.e. to manufacture justification).
- **"A list of things you hadn't considered"** — her image for what a good game produces. The output is a question set, not an answer set.
- **"Cognitive rigidity"** — the failure mode in experts that games are useful for breaking.
- **"Mental models"** — what games perturb.
- **"Surfacing questions"** — what games do that other analytic methods don't.
- **"Negative learning"** — the risk that wargames teach the wrong lesson by reinforcing prior bias, conventional wisdom from the last war, or consensus assumptions. (Used jointly with Heath.)
- **"Quality problems"** — insufficient research, flawed designs, unsound adjudication, missing documentation. Concrete and operational.
- **"Effectiveness gap"** — the absence of empirical evidence that wargaming actually produces learning, creative thinking, or problem solving.
- **"Cluster around a few usual, expected scenarios"** — the failure mode of a wargaming enterprise that doesn't deliberately push off-distribution.
- **"Documentation"** — design rationale, scenario, insights, lessons. She treats lack of documentation as a primary diagnostic of low quality.
- **"Tradecraft"** — preferred over "methodology" when she is talking about practitioner skill.
- **"Empirical research on wargaming"** — the thing that does not yet exist and that her academic-discipline argument is trying to call into being.
- **"Rigor"** — used carefully and operationally; she is in implicit dialogue with Sepinsky's "effectiveness over rigor" rejoinder.
- **"Design thinking"** — present in her 2020 Q&A, but as a discipline imported into game design, not as branding.

She does not speak in big-picture aphorisms. The register is operations-research-adjacent, social-science-precise, mildly skeptical, and allergic to PR language.

## The arguments she makes that our project needs to land in front of

1. **A wargame is a hypothesis generator, not a predictor.** This is the core. Our spec must avoid any language suggesting our LLM-driven adversary moves "predict" PLA behavior or anyone else's. The Adversarial-Distribution Red Team produces candidate moves that the human team has not considered; the "list of things you hadn't considered" framing is exactly her register. Our **explicit separation between insight generation and prediction** in the architecture should be foregrounded as a methodological commitment, not a feature.

2. **Wargames in DoD are routinely consumed as if they were predictions, and worse, as validation of preferred concepts.** Her Wong-and-Heath critique. Our project's value proposition is that an off-distribution generator deliberately *escapes* the comfortable scenario cluster. The README should name the failure mode (games that "cluster around a few usual, expected scenarios" and "validate" service concepts) without name-checking her.

3. **The field has no cumulative empirical knowledge about what makes a wargame produce learning.** Our **audit-logged LLM call store, prompt versioning via git blob hash, and SQLite-backed memory of every adjudication** are the right answer to this concern: every artifact our system produces is reproducible, auditable, and version-pinned. We are building on the side of empirical accumulation. We should describe these features in those terms — as preconditions for accumulation, not as engineering hygiene.

4. **Transparent assumptions are the precondition for legitimate use of wargame outputs.** This shows up across her work and in Rubel's epistemology. Our **prompt files in `src/prompts/*.md`, loaded by filename and pinned by git hash**, make every model assumption inspectable. The doctrine RAG retrieval over PLA documents is similarly inspectable: any move can be traced back to its retrieved support. Frame these as the "transparent assumptions" requirement satisfied at the level of system design.

5. **An off-distribution generator must actually be off-distribution, or it just reinforces priors.** Wong's "negative learning" concern. The CLAUDE.md house rule that "the off-distribution generator must NOT do doctrine RAG retrieval" is the right answer to this argument and should be visible in the spec. Doctrine retrieval is what keeps the Blue analyst grounded; the off-distribution generator's job is to escape the gap, not stay in it. Naming this design choice explicitly is the cleanest signal that we have read this literature.

## Adjacent scholars / community

- **Peter Perla** (CNA) — author of *The Art of Wargaming* (Naval Institute Press, 1990), the foundational reference. Statistician and OR analyst. His 2011 *Naval War College Review* essay with Ed McGrady, "Why Wargaming Works," is the canonical articulation that wargames work through narrative and player engagement, not predictive accuracy. Wong has collaborated with Perla on Project Cassandra; his framing of wargames as generators of "stories" that produce "insight into the dynamics of competitions" is the upstream of her register.
- **Robert C. Rubel** (Naval War College, retired) — author of "The Epistemology of War Gaming," *Naval War College Review* 59:2 (2006). Argues wargames can generate legitimate knowledge but also "valid-looking garbage," and calls for wargaming to evolve from craft to profession. Wong's "academic discipline of wargaming" project is downstream of Rubel's call.
- **Ed McGrady** (CNA, 30+ years; now teaching game design and consulting) — Perla's co-author on "Why Wargaming Works." Frames wargames as stories that illuminate the underlying logic of future interactions rather than predict outcomes. Same tradition as Wong on the "future-oriented wargames do not predict the future" line.
- **Phil Sabin** (King's College London) — *Simulating War* (2012). Academic establishment of wargame design as a research discipline; Sabin's KCL programme is a sister institution to the academic-field-of-wargaming project Wong has championed.
- **Elizabeth M. Bartels** — co-author with Wong on the Marine Corps reports; has written separately on philosophies of science for research games and on multi-method analysis. Influential among wargaming epistemologists for using Jackson's typology of philosophical commitments to differentiate game types.
- **Sebastian Joon Bae**, **Benjamin Michael Smith**, **Christopher Paul** — RAND co-authors on the Marine Corps and information-environment work.
- **Aaron B. Frank** — Wong's co-author on *Deterrence in the Age of Thinking Machines*; published "Gaming AI without AI" in the same JDMS special issue Wong introduced.
- **James Ryseff**, **Nick Riggs** — Wong's co-authors on the 2025 JDMS introduction.
- **Garrett Heath** — co-author on the 2021 *War on the Rocks* DoD-progress critique.
- **Rex Brynen** (PAXsims, McGill) — long-time collaborator and the public-facing voice of the wargaming community; Brynen's gloss on Millennium Challenge is the lens through which the community now reads that case.
- **Jacquelyn Schneider, Benjamin Schechter, Rachael Shaffer** — the experimental-wargaming line at the Hoover Institution / Naval War College; their 2021 *Simulation & Gaming* article on the International Crisis Wargame is adjacent methodological territory.
- **Jeremy Sepinsky** (CNA) — author of "Is It a Wargame? It Doesn't Matter" (*War on the Rocks*, 2021), the principal published rejoinder to Wong and Heath. Argues effectiveness in an ecosystem trumps rigor of design. Worth knowing as the inside-baseball debate Wong is part of.

## Her position (if any) on AI in wargaming

Yuna Wong is currently leading applied work on this question at JHU/APL and curated the JDMS January 2025 special issue. The position visible in the public record:

- She regards AI in wargaming as the inevitable next research frontier and has actively claimed the territory.
- The specific question her APL group is working on is **how well LLMs can replicate an adversary** — i.e. exactly the question our project addresses. This is the sharpest reason to get the framing right in front of her.
- Her introduction to the JDMS special issue acknowledges that the underlying papers were drafted around machine-learning systems and that generative AI / ChatGPT-class tools have changed the question; she is not ideologically attached to any one technique.
- *Deterrence in the Age of Thinking Machines* (2020) flags machine-speed escalation, AI optimizing relative quantitative measures, and inadvertent escalation as concerns. The implicit caution: if you wire LLMs into a wargame as decision-makers, you import all of those problems into the analytic apparatus.
- I found no evidence of a published critique of any specific LLM-in-wargaming research. The community is small and she is its connector; her stance is "do this carefully and document everything," not "don't do this."

She has not, in the public record, endorsed or attacked any specific named system. Treat her as a careful, well-disposed expert who will read our spec for whether we have absorbed the validation/prediction distinction, not for whether we cite her.

## Millennium Challenge 2002 — the right brief framing

Millennium Challenge 2002 was a $250M Joint Forces Command exercise designed to validate post-2010 joint warfighting concepts. The Red commander, retired LtGen Paul Van Riper, used asymmetric tactics — a pre-emptive cruise-missile strike, motorcycle couriers, lights-out signaling — and sank sixteen Blue ships in the opening hours. Rather than analyze the result, the exercise was paused, the ships refloated, and Red's free play constrained until Blue won. The case is canonical in the wargaming-validation literature for what happens when an institution treats a wargame as confirmation of its preferred concept rather than as a generator of uncomfortable hypotheses.

## How our spec should sound to her

**Reinforce the CLAUDE.md house rule: do not name-check Yuna Wong's work in the demo. The register lands without flattery.** Citing her would read as currying favor and would tell her we have read her CV but not her arguments. Use the vocabulary instead.

Concrete advice on language:

- **Use** "hypothesis generator," "off-distribution," "transparent assumptions," "audit log," "prompt version," "insight" (sparingly), "candidate moves," "scenarios the analyst has not considered," "documentation," "tradecraft," "reproducibility," "external validity" (used carefully and only where we mean it).
- **Avoid** "prediction," "forecast," "AI predicts what China will do," "validates the concept," "confirms," "proves," "ground truth" (unless we mean it in the OR sense), and any breathless framing of LLMs as oracles.
- **Make the architecture's epistemology visible.** State that the off-distribution generator has no doctrine retrieval *because its job is to escape the comfortable scenario cluster, not to stay in it*. State that every LLM call is logged with prompt git hash and adjudication trace *because the field needs cumulative empirical evidence about what works and what doesn't*. State that the system separates insight generation from prediction *because that distinction is load-bearing*.
- **Tone:** dry, operational, OR-adjacent. Not enthusiastic. Not visionary. Treat the project as a contribution to wargaming tradecraft, not as a demonstration of AI capability.
- **Do not lecture.** She knows the literature. The signal we want to send is competence, not earnestness.
- **The README should not contain the word "powerful."** It should contain the words "documented," "reproducible," "auditable," and "off-distribution."

If she reads the spec and recognizes the register without seeing her name in the bibliography, we have done it right.
