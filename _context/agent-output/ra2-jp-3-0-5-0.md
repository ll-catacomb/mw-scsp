# RA-2: JP 3-0 / 5-0 Key Passages

> Research-agent deliverable for the Adversarial-Distribution Red Team prototype (SCSP 2026 Wargaming track). Sourced from publicly available, unclassified US JP 3-0 and JP 5-0. Page citations refer to the editions linked below. Where the current edition (JP 3-0, 18 June 2022; JP 5-0, 1 December 2020) renumbers a passage, that is flagged inline. Continuity across editions is high; the doctrinal vocabulary is what the RAG corpus needs to retrieve.

---

## Why these documents matter for our system

JP 3-0 and JP 5-0 are the two keystone joint publications a US Blue planner would actually reach for when adjudicating a PLA Taiwan-contingency move. JP 3-0 supplies the *operations* vocabulary — phasing, centers of gravity, decisive points, lines of operations and effort, branches and sequels, transitions. JP 5-0 supplies the *planning* vocabulary — the Joint Planning Process (JPP), COA development and screening criteria, wargaming as a planning step, problem framing, operational approach, risk assessment. Together they constitute the doctrinal substrate against which a doctrine-grounded LLM should judge the plausibility, sufficiency, and risk of any proposed Blue response.

For our Blue adjudicator, the corpus matters because Blue is *required by doctrine* to evaluate each adversary move along specific axes (COG implications, phase transition risk, decisive-point exposure, branch/sequel triggers) and to use a specific screening grammar (suitable / feasible / acceptable / distinguishable / complete). A Blue adjudicator that cannot speak this grammar reads as out-of-register to a wargame audience. The RAG corpus needs to surface the *exact phrases* the JPP uses, not paraphrases.

For our Red modal-move generator, the corpus matters in a weaker sense. JP 5-0's wargaming step institutionalizes a "most probable / most dangerous" binary for adversary COAs, run through a red cell that "role-plays the adversary commander and staff." This is precisely the templated adversary modeling our prototype is designed to escape. Red's off-distribution branch should *not* be retrieving doctrine; its job is to escape the gap that doctrine creates. We ingest JP 3-0/5-0 so Blue can hold its register, not so Red can stay inside it.

## JP 3-0 key passages

### COG definition and critical-factors framework — Ch. IV, pp. IV-9 to IV-12

> "A COG is the source of moral or physical strength, power, and resistance — what Clausewitz called 'the hub of all power and movement, on which everything depends... the point at which all our energies should be directed.'"
> *(JP 3-0, IV-10)*

> "All COGs have inherent 'critical capabilities' — those means that are considered crucial enablers for the adversary's COG to function and essential to the accomplishment of the adversary's assumed objective(s)... all critical capabilities have essential 'critical requirements' — those essential conditions, resources, and means for a critical capability to be fully operational. Critical vulnerabilities are those aspects or components of the adversary's critical requirements which are deficient or vulnerable to direct or indirect attack..."
> *(JP 3-0, IV-11)*

**Why this matters for the pipeline:** The Blue adjudicator should be able to speak in *critical capability / critical requirement / critical vulnerability* (CC/CR/CV) terms when describing what a PLA move threatens. The Red ensemble's "decapitation" or "fait-accompli" branches map directly to attacks on a friendly strategic COG (alliance cohesion, US public will, Indo-Pacific basing access). RAG retrieval on the phrase "critical vulnerability" should always succeed.

### Operational design elements (the seventeen) — Ch. IV, p. IV-3 to IV-21, Figure IV-3

The doctrinal list a Blue planner is expected to consider: termination; end state and objectives; effects; **center of gravity**; **decisive points**; direct versus indirect approach; **lines of operations** and lines of effort; operational reach; simultaneity and depth; timing and tempo; forces and functions; leverage; balance; anticipation; synergy; culmination; **arranging operations** (which includes phasing). *(JP 3-0, Fig. IV-3, p. IV-6.)*

**Why this matters:** Each Blue adjudication output should reference at least one design element by name. "This PLA move forces an early culmination of our Phase II posture and exposes the alliance COG" is doctrine-grounded; "this is bad for us" is not.

### Decisive points — Ch. IV, p. IV-12

> "A decisive point is a geographic place, specific key event, critical factor, or function that, when acted upon, allows a commander to gain a marked advantage over an adversary or contributes materially to achieving success... Decisive points can be thought of as a way to relate what is 'critical' to what is 'vulnerable.'"
> *(JP 3-0, IV-12)*

**Why this matters:** Many off-distribution Red moves work by *redefining what counts as a decisive point* (e.g., a subsea cable, a port labor union, an Okinawan municipal vote). Blue adjudication should detect when Red has shifted the decisive-point set out from under doctrine.

### Phasing model — Ch. IV, pp. IV-26 to IV-29, Figure IV-7

The six-phase model: **Phase 0 Shape — Phase I Deter — Phase II Seize the Initiative — Phase III Dominate — Phase IV Stabilize — Phase V Enable Civil Authority.** *(JP 3-0, Fig. IV-7, p. IV-27.)*

> "The intent of [the Deter] phase is to deter undesirable adversary action by demonstrating the capabilities and resolve of the joint force... characterized by preparatory actions that specifically support or facilitate the execution of subsequent phases of the operation/campaign."
> *(JP 3-0, IV-28)*

**Why this matters:** Many of our highest-value modal Red moves attack the *transition* between phases — e.g., a quarantine timed to make Phase I Deter slip into Phase II without Blue authorization, or a cyber-blackout sequenced to suppress the indicators that would normally drive Phase III escalation authorities. The phasing model is the doctrinal scaffold against which transition risk is articulated.

> Note: JP 3-0 (2022) replaces the strict six-phase model with a *competition continuum* (cooperation / competition below armed conflict / armed conflict) and de-emphasizes the canonical six phases as a default. Ingest both framings; adjudicators in practice still reach for the six-phase vocabulary.

### Branches and sequels — Ch. IV, p. IV-19 and Executive Summary p. xx

> "The CONOPS describes how the actions of the joint force components and supporting organizations will be integrated, synchronized, and phased to accomplish the mission, including potential branches and sequels."
> *(JP 3-0, p. xx)*

A **branch** is a contingent option built into a base plan to address an alternative situation; a **sequel** is the major operation that follows the current operation, typically responding to its outcome. *(Definition synthesized from JP 3-0 / JP 5-0; the doctrinal pair appears throughout both pubs.)*

**Why this matters:** Every Red move should produce a Blue response that names at least one branch or sequel implication. The audit log can use this as a doctrine-fidelity check.

### Adversary-action vocabulary and the systems perspective — Ch. III, p. III-1 ff.

> "A systems perspective of the operational environment is fundamental to operational design. It considers more than just an adversary's military capabilities, it also strives to provide a perspective of the interrelated systems that comprise the operational environment relevant to a specific joint operation."
> *(JP 3-0, p. xix)*

JP 3-0 instructs Blue to read Red as a system embedded in a system: PMESII (political, military, economic, social, information, infrastructure) is the canonical decomposition.

**Why this matters:** When the off-distribution generator produces a move that crosses PMESII boundaries (e.g., a *military* maneuver designed to produce an *information* effect on an *economic* node), the Blue adjudicator should recognize the cross-domain pattern and not flatten it back into a kinetic-only frame.

## JP 5-0 key passages

### The Joint Planning Process — Ch. III/IV (depending on edition)

The seven-step JPP:

1. Planning Initiation
2. Mission Analysis
3. Course of Action Development
4. **Course of Action Analysis and Wargaming**
5. Course of Action Comparison
6. Course of Action Approval
7. Plan or Order Development

*(JP 5-0 (2006), Fig. III-1, p. III-2; preserved with renumbering in JP 5-0 (2020), Ch. V.)*

**Why this matters:** Blue adjudicator outputs should map cleanly onto these steps. A doctrine-grounded Blue response to a Red move is, in effect, a partial re-execution of Steps 2 → 4: re-frame the problem, generate alternative responses, wargame them.

### COA screening criteria (the Five) — Ch. III, p. III-28, Figure III-6

> "A valid COA is one that is **adequate, feasible, acceptable, distinguishable, and complete**."
> - **Adequate** — Can accomplish the mission within the commander's guidance.
> - **Feasible** — Can accomplish the mission within the established time, space, and resource limitations.
> - **Acceptable** — Must balance cost and risk with the advantage gained.
> - **Distinguishable** — Must be sufficiently different from the other courses of action.
> - **Complete** — Must incorporate objectives, effects, tasks, forces, deployment/employment/sustainment concepts, time estimates, military end state, and mission success criteria.
>
> *(JP 5-0, III-28, Fig. III-6.)*

> "The staff should reject potential COAs that do not meet all five criteria."
> *(JP 5-0, III-28)*

**Why this matters:** This is *the* primary screening grammar the Blue adjudicator must apply when scoring Blue's candidate responses to a Red move. Each adjudication output should produce a per-criterion verdict. The "distinguishable" criterion in particular is what should reject Blue responses that are merely re-skinned versions of one another — useful for catching mode collapse in the Blue ensemble.

> Note: JP 5-0 (2020) replaces *adequate* with *suitable* in the screening criteria, giving **suitable / feasible / acceptable / distinguishable / complete**. Both vocabularies appear in current usage; the corpus should ingest both.

### COA Analysis and Wargaming (Step 4) — Ch. III, pp. III-30 to III-32

> "...the commander should wargame each tentative COA against the **most probable and the most dangerous adversary COAs**... identified through the JIPOE process."
> *(JP 5-0, III-30 to III-31)*

> "Each critical event within a proposed COA should be wargamed based upon time available using the **action, reaction, counteraction** method of friendly and/or opposition force interaction."
> *(JP 5-0, III-31)*

> "A robust cell that can aggressively pursue the adversary's point of view when considering adversary counteraction is essential. This **'red cell' role-plays the adversary commander and staff**... **By trying to win the wargame for the adversary**, the red cell helps the staff fully address friendly responses for each adversary COA."
> *(JP 5-0, III-31)*

**Why this matters — and why it is wrong-in-spirit for our purposes:** This is the doctrinal bedrock of templated adversary modeling. The adversary's COA space is reduced to a binary (*most probable* / *most dangerous*); wargaming proceeds in a fixed action → reaction → counteraction cadence; the red cell tries to win, but only inside the COA set already produced by JIPOE. Off-distribution Red moves are by definition *not* in that set and *not* counteractions in a Blue-initiated cadence. An opening fait-accompli or non-kinetic legal-fiction move escapes the action-reaction-counteraction grammar. This passage should be in the Blue adjudicator's RAG corpus precisely so the adjudicator can recognize when a Red move has slipped the doctrinal frame — and so the audit log can record the gap.

### Assumptions and the limits of templated adversary modeling — Ch. III, p. III-13 ff.

> "Assumptions are made for both friendly and adversary situations. **Commanders and staffs should never assume away adversary capabilities or assume that unrealistic friendly capabilities would be available.**"
> *(JP 5-0, III-13 to III-14)*

> "A valid assumption has three characteristics: it is logical, realistic, and essential for the planning to continue... Assumptions must be continually reviewed to ensure validity."
> *(JP 5-0, III-13)*

**Why this matters — *the* canonical doctrinal hook for our prototype:** "Never assume away adversary capabilities" is the closest thing JP 5-0 contains to an explicit warning about the limits of templated adversary modeling — the doctrinal license for the adversarial-distribution generator. When the Blue adjudicator encounters an off-distribution Red move, it should retrieve this passage and articulate the failure mode as: *Blue planning had assumed Red away in this register*. Mark this passage high-priority for the doctrine RAG index.

### Operational approach and problem framing — Ch. IV, pp. IV-1 to IV-7

> "The operational approach is the commander's description of the broad actions the force must take to achieve the desired military end state."
> *(JP 5-0 (2020), summarized from Ch. IV; in the 2006 edition the equivalent material is at Ch. IV, p. IV-3 ff.)*

JP 5-0 (2020) elevates **problem framing** as a distinct activity preceding COA development — understanding the operational environment, defining the problem, and developing the operational approach (the "design methodology" or *joint planning design*).

**Why this matters:** Off-distribution Red moves frequently force a *re-framing of the problem* rather than a new COA against the existing problem. Blue adjudicator outputs should include a "frame check" — has this Red move just produced a new branch within the current problem, or has it changed the problem itself?

### Risk assessment — Ch. III, p. III-30, item (6); Ch. IV

> "Acceptable — Must balance cost and risk with the advantage gained."
> *(JP 5-0, III-28; criterion within COA validity)*

> COA analysis output explicitly includes "(6) A risk assessment."
> *(JP 5-0, III-30)*

**Why this matters:** Every adjudication output should carry a risk-tier verdict (e.g., *acceptable*, *acceptable with mitigations*, *unacceptable*). This is the natural attachment point for the audit-log's risk fields.

### Branches and sequels in planning — throughout Ch. III and Ch. IV

> "All commanders continue to develop and analyze branches and sequels as required."
> *(JP 5-0, II-12)*

> "[Step 4 wargaming output includes] Identification of plan branches and sequels."
> *(JP 5-0, III-30)*

**Why this matters:** Pairs with the JP 3-0 branches/sequels passage above. If the Red ensemble produces a sequence of moves over multiple turns, Blue adjudication should at minimum identify the move that triggers a branch and the move that demands a sequel; the absence of either is itself a doctrine-fidelity flag.

## Acronyms / terminology glossary

- **COA** — Course of Action. Validated against the suitable/feasible/acceptable/distinguishable/complete criteria.
- **JPP** — Joint Planning Process. The seven-step planning sequence in JP 5-0.
- **JOPES / JPES** — Joint Operation Planning and Execution System (legacy) / Joint Planning and Execution System (current).
- **JIPOE** — Joint Intelligence Preparation of the Operational Environment. Produces the adversary COA set Blue wargames against.
- **CCIR** — Commander's Critical Information Requirement. Subdivides into PIRs and FFIRs.
- **OPLAN / CONPLAN / OPORD** — Operation Plan / Concept Plan / Operation Order. Levels of planning specificity.
- **CCDR** — Combatant Commander (e.g., USINDOPACOM for a Taiwan contingency).
- **JFC** — Joint Force Commander; **JTF** — Joint Task Force.
- **OE** — Operational Environment.
- **COG** — Center of Gravity. Source of power that provides freedom of action, physical strength, and will to fight.
- **CC / CR / CV** — Critical Capabilities / Critical Requirements / Critical Vulnerabilities. COG decomposition.
- **DP** — Decisive Point.
- **LOO / LOE** — Line of Operations (physical) / Line of Effort (logical).
- **FDO** — Flexible Deterrent Option. Phase I (Deter) is built around these.
- **PMESII** — Political, Military, Economic, Social, Information, Infrastructure. Systems-perspective decomposition.
- **DIME(FIL)** — Diplomatic, Informational, Military, Economic (Financial, Intelligence, Law-enforcement). Instruments-of-national-power decomposition.
- **End State / Termination Criteria** — End State: conditions that, when achieved, attain the strategic objectives. Termination Criteria: standards that must be met before conclusion of a joint operation.
- **Branch / Sequel** — Branch: contingent option within the current operation. Sequel: next major operation that follows.
- **Most Probable COA / Most Dangerous COA** — The doctrinal binary against which Blue wargames Red. Our prototype is, in effect, an attack on the sufficiency of this binary.
- **Red Cell** — Element that role-plays the adversary commander and staff during JPP Step 4. Distinct from a "red team."
