# RA-1: PLA Doctrine — Modal Opening-Move Patterns

*Research agent output for SCSP wargaming red-team prototype. All sources are publicly available, unclassified open-source analysis. Compiled April 2026.*

## Executive summary

A modal AI red-team prompted to generate PLA opening moves for a Taiwan Strait crisis circa 2027–2028 will almost certainly converge on a small, tightly clustered distribution: (1) the "Davidson Window" 2027 framing inherited from former INDOPACOM commander Admiral Phil Davidson's 2021 testimony; (2) a taxonomy of four to six options ranging from gray-zone quarantine through full-scale Joint Island Landing Campaign (JILC), almost all of which trace back to the same handful of doctrinal sources (PLA *Science of Military Strategy*, *Science of Campaigns*, *Science of Joint Operations*) and the same handful of analyst voices (Easton, Henley, the CSIS China Power Project, RAND, the annual DoD CMPR); (3) the Spring 2028 framing as one of two seasonal tide windows (March–May, September–October) for amphibious operations, an argument popularized by Ian Easton; and (4) a quantitative envelope built from now-canonical OOB anchors — ~370+ PLAN battle force ships, ~1,500+ short and medium-range conventional ballistic missiles, eight Type 071 LPDs and three to four Type 075 LHDs, ~31 oceangoing civilian RoRos, and ~300 J-20s of the ~3,150-aircraft PLAAF (DoD 2024; CSIS ChinaPower; The Diplomat 2025). The off-distribution surface that genuinely creative red teams should hit lies in (a) cross-domain moves that are not "PLA-on-Taiwan" but "PRC-on-the-Alliance-System," (b) cognitive/political-warfare moves that don't fire a shot, and (c) infrastructure-targeting moves that exploit civilian critical-infrastructure pre-positioning (Volt Typhoon class) rather than kinetic strikes on Taiwan itself.

## Canonical opening-move taxonomy

The modal red-team will sort PLA options along a coercion-to-conquest spectrum, typically with these six to seven categories. Each is well-attested in open-source analysis.

### 1. Gray-zone coercion (sub-quarantine)
**Definition:** Sustained coast-guard, maritime-militia, ADIZ-incursion, drone, sand-dredging, undersea-cable-cutting, and lawfare pressure short of any declared cordon. The dominant frame is "boiling the frog."
**Typical conditions:** Used continuously today; would be intensified at the start of a crisis to test responses and shape the information environment.
**Doctrine sources cited:** CSIS China Power Project (Funaiole, Hart, Lin, et al.), "Signals in the Swarm" data series; Jamestown Foundation analyses on civilian drone incursions; American Enterprise Institute / Critical Threats analysis on a Kinmen short-of-war coercion campaign (Mazza et al.); Soufan Center IntelBrief July 2025 (Soufan Center 2025).

### 2. Quarantine (law-enforcement-led cordon)
**Definition:** A China Coast Guard–led "customs inspection" or "law enforcement" operation that diverts, inspects, or denies maritime/air traffic to and from Taiwan without a declared blockade. The CSIS framework distinguishes this carefully from a blockade because it is law-enforcement-led rather than military-led, designed to assert PRC sovereignty without firing.
**Typical conditions:** Most plausible near-term coercive option; can be scaled, paused, and resumed.
**Doctrine sources cited:** Bonny Lin, Brian Hart, Matthew Funaiole, Samantha Lu, Truly Tinsley — "How China Could Quarantine Taiwan: Mapping Out Two Possible Scenarios," CSIS, June 2024; Studeman's policy writing on PRC coercion options at MITRE/War on the Rocks (Studeman 2024).

### 3. Full blockade (military-led)
**Definition:** Declared military blockade combining PLAN surface and submarine fleet, PLAAF and Naval Aviation, conventional Rocket Force fires for port and airfield denial, and air-defense umbrella. CSIS identifies six force types in a joint blockade: PLAN surface, PLAN submarines, air forces (PLAAF + Naval Aviation + Army Aviation), conventional Rocket Force, coastal/air defense, and support forces.
**Typical conditions:** Either as a coercive endgame or as the precursor/cover for invasion.
**Doctrine sources cited:** CSIS ChinaPower "How China Could Blockade Taiwan" interactive; Lonnie Henley, USCC testimony (Feb 2021), and CMSI China Maritime Report No. 26, "Beyond the First Battle: Overcoming a Protracted Blockade of Taiwan" (Henley 2023). Henley's central argument: "any major conflict against Taiwan will include a full-scale air, maritime, and information blockade of the island… defeating the landing does not end the conflict" (Henley 2021).

### 4. Joint Firepower Strike Campaign (missile and air campaign against C2 and infrastructure)
**Definition:** Concentrated strikes by the PLA Rocket Force, PLAAF, and PLAN cruise-missile platforms against Taiwan's command and control, air bases, naval bases, radars, integrated air defenses, and critical civilian infrastructure (power, water, ports). PLA's *Science of Joint Operations* identifies the Joint Firepower Strike Operation (JFSO) as designed to "intimidate an adversary's leadership and population, break its will to resist, and force it to abandon or reverse its strategic intentions."
**Typical conditions:** Either independently, as escalation; or as the opening salvo of an invasion.
**Doctrine sources cited:** Annual DoD CMPR (2024 and 2025 editions); China Aerospace Studies Institute (CASI) PLA Rocket Force toolkit; Mihal, "Understanding the People's Liberation Army Rocket Force," *Military Review* (Mihal 2021); CSIS ChinaPower "How Are China's Land-based Conventional Missile Forces Evolving?"

### 5. Decapitation strike
**Definition:** Targeted strikes (cruise/ballistic missiles, special operations forces, possibly drones) against Taiwan's senior political-military leadership, communications, and key government nodes. Open-source reporting documents PLA training facilities in northern China that include replicas of Taiwan's Presidential Office Building (CCTV 2015 imagery), and the SCMP reports the training area has expanded threefold since 2020.
**Typical conditions:** As opening move of a "short, sharp" invasion; Easton notes that if decapitation fails, the PLA may need to land 300,000–400,000 troops to seize key objectives.
**Doctrine sources cited:** Ian Easton, *The Chinese Invasion Threat* (Project 2049, 2017); Easton, "Hostile Harbors" (Project 2049, 2021); Global Taiwan Institute analyses on PLA SOF.

### 6. Outlying-island seizure (Kinmen, Matsu, Dongsha/Pratas, Wuchiu)
**Definition:** Limited amphibious or air-mobile operation to seize one or more of Taiwan's outlying islands as a coercive gambit short of attacking Taiwan proper. Kinmen sits 2 miles from Xiamen; Matsu 6 miles from the PRC mainland; Dongsha is in the South China Sea and is described as "easily capturable" by the 2024 DoD CMPR.
**Typical conditions:** As a low-cost test of US/allied resolve; as a fait accompli to fracture Taiwan politically; or as a stepping stone.
**Doctrine sources cited:** DoD CMPR 2024; AEI/Critical Threats short-of-war Kinmen scenario (Mazza et al. 2024); 2025 DIA Worldwide Threat Assessment (cited in Army Recognition 2025); Heritage Foundation analyses.

### 7. Full-scale Joint Island Landing Campaign (JILC) — invasion
**Definition:** PLA's main operational concept for cross-strait invasion. The PLA's *Science of Campaigns* describes three phases: (1) preliminary operations (information dominance, fires, blockade); (2) assembly, embarkation, and transit; (3) assault landing and beachhead establishment. The doctrine has six pillars per CMSI/Diplomat synthesis: dominance of three domains (air/sea/info), key-point strikes, concentration of "elite strengths," rapid and continuous assaults, integrated and flexible support operations, and psychological attacks.
**Typical conditions:** When PRC leadership concludes coercion has failed and the cost of inaction exceeds the cost of war; conditioned heavily by season (see "Spring 2028 timing framing").
**Doctrine sources cited:** *Science of Military Strategy* (Academy of Military Science); *Science of Campaigns*; *Science of Joint Operations*; CMSI China Maritime Reports #19 (PLA Airborne Corps in JILC), #21 (Civilian Shipping and Maritime Militia), #26 (Beyond the First Battle); CSIS, "The First Battle of the Next War" (Cancian, Cancian, Heginbotham 2023); RAND, "Tallying the U.S.-China Military Scorecard" (Heginbotham et al. 2015).

## Capability and OOB anchors

The modal red-team will reach for these now-canonical numbers. Each is sourced; date-stamped where the source is explicit.

### PLA Navy
- **Total battle force ~370+ ships, projected to ~395 by 2025 and ~435 by 2030** (DoD CMPR 2024). USNI News reported the prior-year DoD projection of "400 ships by 2025" emphasizing growth in major surface combatants (USNI News, Nov 2022).
- **~60+ multirole destroyers** centered on Type 055 (Renhai) — 8 active, with a second batch under the 14th Five-Year Plan — and Type 052D (Luyang III) — ~25 active (Wikipedia / open-source compilations cross-referenced with DoD CMPR 2024).
- **~50 modern frigates** including ~35+ Type 054A (Jiangkai II); plus ~50 corvettes (Type 056/056A Jiangdao class) (open-source compilations 2024–2025).
- **Submarine force:** ~60+ submarines including 6 Jin-class SSBNs and ~6 Shang-class SSNs, growing toward Type 093B and Type 095 (DoD CMPR 2024).

### PLA Rocket Force (conventional inventory)
The DoD CMPR 2024 inventory figures (which the modal LLM will quote from):
- **~900 conventional short-range ballistic missiles (SRBM)**
- **~1,300 conventional medium-range ballistic missiles (MRBM)**
- **~500 conventional intermediate-range ballistic missiles (IRBM)** — DF-26 family (~250–400 launchers, multiple brigades)
- **~400 ground-launched cruise missiles (GLCM)**
- **DF-17 hypersonic glide vehicle** — deployed to at least three brigades (CSIS ChinaPower)
- **DF-21D ASBM** — inventory grew from ~6 to ~30 launchers between 2013 and 2020 (CSIS ChinaPower / DoD CMPR)

### Amphibious lift (the doctrinal hard constraint)
- **8 Type 071 LPDs** (Yuzhao class)
- **3 Type 075 LHDs** in service, with a fourth and the larger Type 076 (Sichuan, with electromagnetic catapult) entering trials (Naval News 2024–2025)
- **~32 LSTs**, fewer than 50 smaller landing ships, **6 Zubr-class** and **15+ Yuyi-class** hovercraft (open-source compilations)
- **~10 Type 726/726A LCACs** in service (4 fit in a Type 071 well deck; 2 in a Type 075)
- **~31 oceangoing civilian RoRo ferries** identified as available for sealift augmentation (Naval News 2023; Henley CMSI Report #21)
- **The amphibious-lift constraint is the modal red team's favorite "the PLA can't quite yet" anchor.** Standard analytic claim: organic PLAN amphibious lift moves perhaps one heavy combined-arms brigade per wave; civilian RoRo augmentation is the swing factor that takes the JILC from infeasible to plausible.

### PLA Air Force
- **Total ~3,150 aircraft** with a "great leap forward in combat aircraft" reported through 2025 (The Diplomat, Nov 2025).
- **J-20 stealth fighter:** grew from ~50 airframes in 2020 to ~300 by mid-2025; J-20A/J-20S variants entering service (The Diplomat 2025).
- **J-16 multirole:** ~450 by 2025.
- **J-10 (all variants):** ~550 by 2025.
- These three modern types together are the modal anchor; the modal red team will cite "the PLAAF now operates the world's largest air force in Asia and the third-largest globally" from the DoD CMPR.

### Operational tempo (forcing-function indicator)
- INDOPACOM commander Admiral Samuel Paparo testified that PLA military pressure on Taiwan rose ~300% in 2024 (cited in USCC 2024 Annual Report and Breaking Defense 2025).
- Joint Sword 2024A: 46 PLAN vessels around Taiwan over two days (CSIS ChinaPower).
- Joint Sword 2024B (October 2024): 153 aircraft sorties, 111 ADIZ intrusions (CSIS ChinaPower).
- December 2024 exercise: ~90 ships from East China Sea to South China Sea, the largest in nearly 30 years (USCC 2024).

## Spring 2028 timing framing

The "Spring 2028" framing inherits from two converging arguments:

1. **The "Davidson Window."** Former INDOPACOM commander Admiral Phil Davidson testified to the Senate Armed Services Committee in March 2021 that the threat to Taiwan would manifest "in this decade, in fact in the next six years," establishing 2027 as the canonical inflection year. The 2024 DoD CMPR documents Xi's reported tasking that the PLA be able to invade Taiwan by end of 2027 (DoD 2024). Notably, USNI News (March 2026) reports the latest US intelligence community assessment that Beijing has not committed to a 2027 invasion and has no fixed unification timeline — a useful counterweight, but the modal red team will still default to the 2027 framing.

2. **Tide and weather windows.** Open-source analysis (Easton in particular) holds that the Taiwan Strait's seasonal weather narrows amphibious feasibility to two windows: roughly **late March through May**, and **September through October**, with **April and October** being the canonical "Goldilocks" months. Outside these windows the strait sees the East Asian monsoon (summer May–September) and the Northeast monsoon (winter November–March), with prohibitive sea states, frequent typhoons, and torrential rain that degrade landing-craft and small-boat operations and make sustained logistics hazardous. This argument is most associated with **Ian Easton** in *The Chinese Invasion Threat* and the related Project 2049 work.

**Spring 2028 specifically** therefore lands inside the optimal April window (a) just past the 2027 readiness target and (b) in a year where the political calendar (Taiwan's 2028 presidential election in January, US transition dynamics, etc.) creates reasonable scenario hooks. The modal red team will pick March–May 2028 for amphibious phase activity if invasion is scenarioed; otherwise it will scenario the buildup as "covered by exercises" in winter 2027–2028 with execution timed to the spring window.

**Open-source authors who write specifically on the tide-window argument:**
- **Ian Easton** — primary popularizer of the April/October framing. *The Chinese Invasion Threat* (2017); "Hostile Harbors" (2021); commentary in *Taiwan News*, "Window closed on Chinese invasion of Taiwan during US election: Easton" (Oct 2020). [unverified — Easton's specific tide-window section locations: I confirmed Easton uses the dual-window framing in interviews and the book is widely cited for it, but did not pull exact page numbers.]
- **War on the Rocks** treatments: "A Strait Too Far" (June 2023) — discusses Pacific campaigning approach with reference to seasonal constraints.
- **CIMSEC / *Lethal Minds Journal*** popular treatments echo the April/October framing.
- **Frontiers in Marine Science**, "Land-Ocean Interaction Affected by the Monsoon Regime Change in Western Taiwan Strait" (2021) — physical oceanography backing the operational claim.
- **ASPI Strategist**, "Floating piers and sinking hopes: China's logistics challenge in invading Taiwan" (2024) — extends the seasonal/logistics argument.

## Off-distribution candidates

Moves that the modal LLM ensemble is likely to MISS or under-weight, with sources where they exist. These are seeds for the red-team's "what would surprise the model" set.

1. **Distributed civil-maritime presence using fishing fleet as primary instrument.** The maritime militia is well-attested (Erickson 2024; CSIS 2025), but the modal LLM treats it as gray-zone *coercion* rather than as the *main effort*. Off-distribution version: PRC declares a "civil rescue and humanitarian zone" and floods the strait with thousands of fishing vessels, putting the burden of escalation on Taiwan/US to attack civilians. (Sources: CSIS "Signals in the Swarm"; Erickson "Missing in the Gray Zone?"; Henley CMSI #21.)

2. **Coordinated cyber against CONUS logistics nodes (Volt Typhoon class action) as the opening shot, before any move on Taiwan.** PLA cyber pre-positioning in US civilian rail, port, water, and energy networks is publicly documented (CISA/FBI Volt Typhoon advisories; *Air & Space Forces Magazine* 2024–2025; Unit 42; NJCCIC). Off-distribution: PRC goes first against US homeland logistics and *only then* moves on Taiwan, knowing that INDOPACOM force flow is degraded. (Sources: https://unit42.paloaltonetworks.com/volt-typhoon-threat-brief/; https://www.airandspaceforces.com/pentagon-civilian-infrastructure-deploy-china/.)

3. **Kinetic action against Japan-based logistics rather than Taiwan directly.** The modal LLM scenarios center on Taiwan; a creative red team would scenario PLA Rocket Force conventional fires aimed at Yokota, Kadena, Iwakuni, Sasebo, or the seven SDF–US logistics nodes — and treat Taiwan as a fait accompli to be claimed afterwards. (Source: implicit in *First Battle of the Next War* sensitivity runs but not foregrounded; RAND Scorecard treats this domain.)

4. **Weaponizing Taiwan's domestic political fractures rather than overt force.** PRC political warfare leveraging Taiwan's KMT/DPP polarization, election interference, financial coercion of Taiwanese business in PRC, and "integration" frames around Kinmen. (Source: Studeman testimony to CECC, July 2025; Global Taiwan Institute, "China Initiates a New Integration Model with Taiwan, Starting with Gray Zone Tactics towards Kinmen"; Interpopulum, "Securitizing Kinmen.") Modal LLM will mention this but under-weight it as a *primary* lever.

5. **Dual-use space asset action below kinetic threshold.** Co-orbital inspection/grappling, GPS jamming, tactical SATCOM denial, or "accidental" laser dazzling of US space-based ISR over the strait — all below the threshold that triggers a treaty response. (Source: CSIS Aerospace Security Project, *Space Threat Assessment* annual; Secure World Foundation *Global Counterspace Capabilities* annual; not strongly featured in PLA-on-Taiwan modal corpus.)

6. **Submarine-emplaced cable cuts at chokepoints OUTSIDE the strait (e.g., undersea cables connecting Guam, Saipan, the Philippines) as the first kinetic move.** This is a "everything goes dark" opener that doesn't touch Taiwan. (Source: Matsu cable-cut incidents 2023 are documented; broader Indo-Pacific cable warfare is under-explored in PLA-on-Taiwan corpus.)

7. **Fait accompli on Dongsha (Pratas) atoll combined with a public, conditional offer.** Seize Dongsha, then publicly offer to "withdraw within 30 days" if Taiwan accepts a specific political arrangement. Forces alliance to choose between escalation over a 1.7 sq km atoll and acquiescence. (Source: 2025 DIA WTA via Army Recognition; DoD CMPR 2024 notes Dongsha is "easily capturable.")

8. **Targeted attack on Taiwan's energy import dependency rather than military infrastructure.** Taiwan imports ~98% of its energy. A precision campaign against LNG terminals, regasification ships, and the LNG SLOC south of Taiwan creates a 7–10 day "lights out" without any landing. (Source: FDD, "Chinese Coercion of Taiwan's Energy Lifelines," Nov 2025.)

9. **Cognitive-warfare deepfake of Taiwan leadership announcing surrender, timed to a real cyber blackout.** Modal LLM will mention "info ops" but rarely scenario a coordinated info-cyber-fait-accompli triple. (Source: implicit in Taiwan MND analyses on cognitive warfare; Soufan Center 2025.)

10. **Coordinated North Korean conventional or nuclear demonstration timed to absorb US/ROK attention.** Multi-front opportunism that the modal Taiwan-focused LLM will simply not include because its priors are "PLA versus Taiwan." (Source: not in the PLA Taiwan corpus per se; would need DPRK-focused sources merged in.)

11. **PRC-claimed environmental/climate emergency as cover for a "humanitarian flotilla" through the strait.** A pretextual frame that converts a quarantine into "rescue operations" — putting Taiwan in the position of refusing aid. (Source: not in modal corpus; speculative seed.)

12. **Anti-shipping action against neutral-flagged tankers in the South China Sea or Malacca Strait, attributed to "rogue actors," to spike global insurance markets and pressure US/EU politically before any move on Taiwan.** (Source: Indo-Pacific maritime security analyses; not in PLA-on-Taiwan modal corpus.)
