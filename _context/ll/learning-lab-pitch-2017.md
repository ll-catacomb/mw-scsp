# What is the Learning Lab?

**Date:** January 17, 2017

The Bok Center's "Learning Lab" is a space designed to house and incubate faculty-driven projects related to teaching and learning. This involves designing and building courses, materials, assignments and activities for faculty, and providing support and space for the students who are encountering these new "learning objects" for the very first time.

And since these new teaching and learning activities often involve unconventional modes of communicating, making, and creating—data visualization, film-making, public speaking, 3D modeling, virtual reality, sculpture, dance and performance, public service, among many many others—the Learning Lab offers a space in which faculty and students can develop their skills in these multiple media with the help of expert staff, including artists and technologists in residence.

## Teaching and Learning Intertwined

Central to the Learning Lab is the sense that teaching and learning are deeply intertwined, that, as Dewey put it,

> Teaching and learning are correlative or corresponding processes, as much so as selling and buying. One might as well say he [or she] has sold when no one has bought, as to say that he [or she] has taught when no one has learned. And in the educational transaction, the initiative lies with the learner even more than in commerce it lies with the buyer.

This idea plays itself out in the Learning Lab's work in three key ways:

1. It follows that it might not be the best idea to engage in the instructional design process in isolation from learners. Of course, in practice, we engage in one on one consultations with faculty as we design learning objects, working with the faculty member's classroom experience as a good source of intuitions on ways students *might* respond the new idea we're pursuing, but our goal is *to as quickly as possible deliver a working prototype to actual learners for feedback* (our Learning Lab Undergraduate Fellows [see below]).

2. If Dewey's teacher can't say she has taught if no one has learned, then a teaching and learning center can't say that it has supported teaching if a) all it has offered a faculty member is consultation and design help before any actual teaching takes place, and, b) if it has focussed solely on the faculty development side of the equation, supporting the faculty member's work rather than the students' learning. A meeting in July can produce materials with great potential for teaching, a training for graduate student TFs can produce scholars who are good teachers in potentia, but even if all this happens we have not yet supported teaching *and learning.* So what we have learned in the Learning Lab is that incubating innovations in teaching and learning involves supporting not only the teachers during the design phase, but supporting both teachers and learners during the first year of the implementation phase as well.

3. If we only know that teaching has happened once we know that students have learned, then the process of assessing students' acquisition of knowledge, skills, and attitudes takes on special—even central—importance. For this reason, the Bok Center's Research and Assessment Team is central to each Learning Lab Project, pursuing the goal not of "judging" or "grading" the Professor, but, rather, of assessing the Learning Lab's work in supporting the teachers and learners' activities.

Put simply, experiments in teaching can't happen in a vacuum—they are, simultaneously, experiments in learning, and learners need to be involved and central in the beginning, the middle, and the end.

## The Process

When the Learning Lab works with a course, the process usually begins at least a term (even a year) in advance with meetings between the Learning Lab's staff and the faculty member. And while the trajectory from that point on may vary, we usually find ourselves engaging in the following steps.

### 1. Researching and Sketching

The first step is discovering what's already out there and working with the clients to put together some initial ideas—sketches—of what we might like to try.

### 2. Prototyping & Testing

Once we have something (whether it's a lecture or an assignment or a classroom activity or a syllabus) that we could imagine offering a student, we give over to the Learning Lab Undergraduate Fellows to test. The "LLUFs" then offer us feedback which we record (both in text and on camera if appropriate), and then provide to the teaching staff. We cycle through the prototyping and testing process until the collective judgment of the Learning Lab's teachers and learners feels that we have something that's ready for the Harvard College Classroom.

### 3. Delivering & Supporting

Based on our own experiences performing the new assignments and activities in the Learning Lab, we have a strong sense of the support students will require when the course runs. This often involves:

a) producing well-written prompts, guides, models and rubrics that can be hosted on the course website,

b) offering dedicated workshops on any new skills or tools required for the course, and,

c) hosting hackathons during which students can make use of Learning Lab materials (video studio, art supplies, 3D printers, etc.) and staff expertise (in the form of Graduate Media Fellows and LLUFs) to finish their final projects on site.

It bears emphasizing that we are developing a model of integrated support for faculty and students. As opposed to consulting with faculty in a vacuum and blindly hoping that the units and services that support students (media labs, libraries, peer tutors) will be able to support the faculty's experimental activities, we are offering an entire "stack" of support that targets both the teachers and the learners. When staff know the instructor's goals (because they co-created them with the instructor) they are in a much better position to support students as they attempt to achieve these goals.

### 4. Assessing & Revising

The Bok Center's Research and Assessment team meets with the faculty to develop targeted assessment protocols involving anything from special questions on the Q to focus groups. And the resulting reports guide any future revisions to the course materials and practices we develop.

### 5. Reporting and Curating

The final step involves telling the story of what just happened. Too often what happens in the classroom happens in secret—not by design, but by accident. The Learning Lab operates with an unusual degree of transparency, publishing a constant stream of photos, videos, and text that offer a window on its activities. The work of students enrolled in Harvard College classes remains hidden, of course, but the rest of the participants agree to make their work public as a condition of their involvement, and we hope that this transparency will help to disseminate and celebrate the work of Harvard's faculty in the classroom.

## The People

The Learning Lab is comprised of an intergenerational community of teachers and learners: some are professors, some are students; some are employed by the Bok Center, some tied to a course; many come from the academic world, some from industry or the arts. More than the projects, more than the flashy equipment and its products, it is the people—and the whirl of activity and excitement they generate in our space on a daily basis—that define the Learning Lab. The following categories can help to map out some of the key roles.

### The Clients

**Faculty:** the prime movers for any given project are the faculty—they don't necessarily need to create everything, but they need to sign off on everything; they naturally provide content-area expertise and intellectual leadership on each project.

**Teaching Fellows:** during the implementation phase we work closely with the teaching fellows, as they are typically the ones who directly assign, support and evaluate the students' work.

**Undergraduate Students:** while the course runs (for at least the first iteration of any project) we work extremely closely with the undergraduates enrolled in a course, offering them training, technical support, materials, space, even feedback where appropriate.

### The Learning Lab Team

**Faculty Fellows:** docked with the Bok Center, our faculty fellows provide leadership that extends beyond their own project, offering advice on other faculty projects, helping us design and deliver workshops, materials, Exploratory Seminars, and public events.

**Graduate Student Fellows:** we employ a variety of graduate fellows at the Bok Center, with the "Learning Lab Graduate Fellows" and the "Media, Literacy and Visualization Fellows" the two varieties most deeply involved with the Learning Lab.

**Learning Lab Undergraduate Fellows:** at the heart of the organization are the "LLUFs", undergrads who test prototypes of assignments and activities, offer feedback and ideas in response, and, in many cases, work collaborative with senior Learning Lab staff and the faculty as we revise, deliver, support and assess our projects.

**Bok Staff:** the Bok Center's full time staff is not large, but it plays a key role in facilitating the work of the rest of the community. We provide expertise in pedagogy and instructional design, in assessment, in the many modes and media of communication that are new or unfamiliar to faculty and students, and we design, maintain, and continually improve the workflows and technical infrastructure that keeps the Learning Lab machine running.

**Artists and Technologists:** often the faculty hope to achieve things that lie beyond the current skillset of the team, and in such cases we are happy to bring in outside talent to help on a project by project basis. But, here too, we insist that any visiting artists, technologists and craftspeople not simply work as temporary contractors that deliver nothing but the product. Rather, we invite them to dock with us and build the project in residence, to deliver workshops for Harvard's teachers and learners, to mentor and advise undergraduate and graduate students who are interested in their work.

## The Space, the Tools, & the Media

So, concretely, what does this all look like now? And what would its ultimate incarnation look like?

How can we begin supporting more courses in flight, while simultaneously continuing to facilitate the acquisition of new digital and artistic skills for students and faculty in workshops, while simultaneously ramping up the prototyping and design cycles we run in our design studio?

The Learning Lab's current footprint is small: a 25x25' studio used for both art-making and digital media production, an 25x25' open space and a small classroom used for meetings and hackathons, and the Director's Office, which currently serves as a team room for the Learning Lab undergraduate fellows. We have an array of tools and materials, from cameras, computers and 3D scanning/printing equipment to workbenches and art making supplies, but we are running out of space.

The constraints we face have compelled us to develop the capability to rapidly change—even to relocate—our various styles of studio space, whether to offer a media- or materials-intensive workshop at the Allston Portal or to film a presentation in a laboratory or library for HarvardX. And while this has been born of necessity, we now see that this flexibility is one of our key assets—a defining feature, even—and we intend to maintain this ethos of dynamic flexibility (if not nomadism) as we think about move into larger, more stable spaces.

So the argument is not that we install anything permanent in our new space, but, rather, that we dedicate the space to flexible, dynamic, and experimental use . . . akin to the "black box theatre" notion that originally inspired the SciBox. The Bok Center's staff can erect a pop-up video studio for a week of student presentations in a large intro-science class, then break this down and install a pop-up up art studio or computer-lab to facilitate other modes of making and doing.

This flexibility will ensure that we remain responsive to the changing needs of faculty and students.

But we do need space to house the materials (everything from lights and cameras to brushes and paper) and keep them available to faculty and students.

## The Learning Lab and the Library

For the better part of seven centuries, the curriculum of Western universities revolved almost entirely around guided reading. Year after year, generation after generation, students arrived on campus expecting to be taught how to "read" a variety of subjects—to be taught, in other words, how to identify the canonical books in a range of fields and to understand the ideas within them. The roles of university faculty and librarians in such a curriculum were admirably clear. Faculty, through their lectures—a term itself derived from "lectio," "to read"—modeled the kind of reading which students were expected to do, framing the contributions of the authors on the syllabus and explicating key passages to enrich the students' understanding. Librarians, as the custodians and curators of the most valuable treasure the university contained, were the faculty's indispensable partners in ushering students into the ranks of the well-read.

The radical transformation of the university, effected in no small part by dramatic changes in the relative prestige of different media of communication, has unsettled these once-stable roles with breathtaking speed. It is no longer the case that students—or faculty—assume that the point of college is, essentially, to go and have the smartest people tell you which books to read and how to think about them. In response, voices both within and outside of the university (including academic administrators, foundations, and legislators) often have urged faculty and librarians to look outside of academia for models for how they might reconceptualize their roles and reorganize their labor to suit an ostensible "university of the future." The advent of MOOCs, of libraries remodeled into "information commons" or reimagined as host organisms for data- or digital humanities centers, all represent efforts by faculty and librarians to import modes of curation, creation, and communication pioneered by industry "disruptors" like 24-hour news channels or Silicon Valley start-ups. Common to all of these projects of re-invention is the assumption that faculty and librarians (and universities in general) ought to meet the needs of their students and their societies by reacting to and borrowing from practices developed outside the academy—that is, that it is the university's job to meet its customers "where they are."

What if, instead, the library reasserted itself as a place that generates new modes of student learning? That incubates and nurtures new modes of faculty communication? That did not merely cater to its faculty and student users, but that actively involved them in designing new fields of knowledge, and new ways of disseminating them?

New Cabot's close proximity to the future location of the Bok Center offers singular opportunities for integrating the curricular design and experimentation taking place in the Bok Center's Learning Lab with the resources—both material and human—of the library. To truly scale this effort, partnership with the library is required. The library has long been the University's central node for information, communication, and literacy development, and the Bok Center can be a unique partner as it seeks to remain so.
