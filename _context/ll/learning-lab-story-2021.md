# The Story of the Learning Lab

**Date:** 2021

## The Launch

Early in his tenure as faculty director, Rob Lue wrote the following vision for the Bok Center:

1. Harvard teaching should express the same level of rigor, energy, creativity and leadership that is found in Harvard scholarship and research.
2. Progress in teaching and learning requires an iterative cycle of experimentation followed by data gathering, analysis, and revision.
3. Teaching and learning at Harvard must be deeply connected with the wider world.
4. Teaching is a collaborative effort based on an intergenerational community of faculty, graduate students, and undergraduates.

In spring 2015, Rob launched the Learning Lab as part of a strategy to implement these principles. The goal was to create a space within the Bok Center that provided dynamic, creative opportunities focused on innovative teaching within Harvard's brick-and-mortar classes. The Learning Lab would function as an incubator for projects related to teaching: for faculty interested in designing innovative courses, new modes of instructional content, new assignments and activities for their students, and new types of inclusive and equitable learning environments.

## The Space and the Tools

We turned a small room in the Science Center (and now a large one at 50 Church St) into the "Learning Lab Studio," built by members of the Bok Center who had worked with Rob at HarvardX. This studio was unlike the other video studios his team had helped design (the HarvardX Studio, the Widener Studio) in that rather than being a quiet, controlled room away from noisy activity, it was itself the hub of activity. It was a maker space, as well as a classroom, surrounded by and integrated with a video studio.

The studio and staff grew and developed in response to faculty and student needs and desires. Some of the needs were for tools and materials: faculty needed video production to support their courses, so we built a video studio; they needed paper prototyping supplies, so we filled our shelves with cheap but plentiful card stock. Other needs were for skills, talents, and expertise. We learned that the value we were able to add to the College lay not solely in our technical skills (there are many other talented video production teams on campus, for instance), but in the intersection of our technical and pedagogical expertise.

For example, Peter der Manuelian from NELC wanted us to help his student build 3D museums, so we developed expertise with 3D modeling software, but we also brought in curators from the Harvard Art Museum to consult on the decisions that go into the act of curation. Kaya Williams from Anthropology wanted us to support students who were designing games that offered deep critiques of the prison complex, so we developed expertise on game design, but it was equally essential to integrate Bok Center staff who were experienced in teaching subject matter with great political, ethical and emotional significance while maintaining an inclusive classroom environment.

Over the past five years we have developed our team's expertise in video, 2D and 3D animation, data visualization, graphic design, podcasting, game design, public speaking, and many more media and modes of communication. This has enabled us to create nearly anything a faculty member can dream up, whether it's an interactive web-based game like Music51's "Chord Crusher" or Ned Hall's "Knights and Knaves," or a 15-angle studio record of the Parker Quartet so that students can analyze the bodily movements of performers, or an animation that flies through a topographical map of Turkey to show how patterns of seismic stress develop before and after earthquakes. And, crucially, it has enabled us to help countless undergraduates begin to develop these skills themselves, in the context of their courses.

## The Fellows

It soon became clear that we could not support so many faculty in so many different disciplines with so many pedagogical and technical needs with our full-time staff alone. We needed additional help. Rather than relying on contractors, for instance, we chose to rely on graduate student fellows (initially Learning Lab Graduate Fellows, now the Media and Design Fellows). This work requires a unique combination of disciplinary and pedagogical skills, combined with technical skills (which we help them learn) and a passion for teaching and learning. While it may have been slightly more efficient, perhaps, to hire a web developer to create a 3D viewer for Stephanie Pierce in OEB, there is no way we would have ended up with the magnificent tool that graduate fellow Phil Fahn-Lai created, literally in the hours before the University closed due to COVID, so that students would still be able to view and study key specimens from the lab. Moreover, Phil and our other graduate fellows are developing the cutting edge communication skills that they will require as future faculty.

The other key position we created was the Learning Lab Undergraduate Fellow—or "LLUF"—position. Initially intended as usability testers, the LLUFs tried out all of the new assignments and activities we developed, giving us feedback on what worked and what didn't. What became immediately apparent was that they were far from passive participants in the design process; instead, they became full partners with graduate fellows and faculty, co-designing many of our most interesting projects. Moreover, since they needed to learn the media and tools the activities involved before the courses ran, by the time the "real" students showed up in the Learning Lab for workshops and hackathons, the LLUFs were ready to serve as peer tutors.

The LLUF position enables undergraduates to have a voice, to take ownership of (and responsibility for) their education. We have found their contributions invaluable. While they aren't yet experts in the disciplines, they are experts of their own experience—they know how they learn, they know what makes them feel included or excluded, they know what matters to them. Crucially, they are what economists might call a "leading indicator" of where Harvard is headed, and we have found that in listening to them and giving them a voice, we end up anticipating important issues in higher education, and being more prepared to help when called upon. On a host of issues from social media, to sharing pronouns in class, to anti-racist pedagogies, we have found that the LLUFs are valuable as guides rather than pupils.

## Equity, inclusion, and change

Faculty often seek assistance from us with the explicit objective of creating a more inclusive learning environment. In numerous cases, the Learning Lab has created resources to support students who might lack requisite knowledge, such as interactive tutorials on foundational concepts for Rob Wood's Gen Ed course, and explainer videos for Ed Glaeser's microeconomics course and for the introductory, non-majors physics courses. Moreover, for any new assignment modality, our team offers tailored support that might include workshops, hackathons, and customized web resources, thus ensuring that a student's prior experience is not a barrier.

We have found that faculty and students are often drawn to new media and modes of communication when they want their ideas to go beyond Harvard's gates, to change the world. Our support ranges from Mindich courses that wish to share the outcomes of collaborative projects with neighboring communities, to academics seeking to communicate ideas to the public (Harvard Horizons, Star-Friedman Challenge for Promising Scientific Research, Aramont Fellows), to student groups invested in making new voices heard (Women's Center Gender 101, Black Arts Festival, Soul Sessions, Deaf Awareness Club's Why I Sign, FYRE).

More than one professor, in explaining their goals, has quoted Marx's thesis on Feuerbach, "Philosophers have hitherto only interpreted the world in various ways; the point is to change it." Teaching is quite literally the process of changing minds. If you succeed as a teacher, the student's mind is different. We need to reach students where they are, in the media that will move them—whether they are students in a class, or people beyond Harvard needing to learn about topics ranging from the effectiveness of vaccines to the ineffectiveness of the prison system. The Bok Center's Learning Lab has developed the skills and tools that can help change the world by changing minds, and has become a hub for a community of students and faculty invested in this goal.

What Rob envisioned in 2015 has been realized as both a maker space for paper prototyping and a film studio; both a place where faculty can find grading rubrics and a web-design studio that can build custom web-apps; both an event space and a Multimodal-Communication-Program along the lines of the Writing Program that teaches undergraduates and graduate students new media literacies. Moreover, it is a place that abounds with energy and creatively; that relies on iterative cycles of experimentation; that treats teaching and learning as a collaborative, intergenerational effort; and that is deeply connected with the wider world.

## Learning Lab Services — An Overview

The Learning Lab team collaborates with Harvard faculty to explore, design and build materials, assignments, and activities for Harvard courses. These activities often involve unconventional modes of communicating, making, and creating – including data visualization, film-making, public speaking, 3D modeling, virtual reality, sculpture, dance and performance, and public service, among many others. Since students will often be engaging these media and tools for the very first time, the Learning Lab provides direct instruction to students through workshops and hackathons hosted in our Learning Lab Studio, a flexible space with dynamic technological capabilities and artmaking supplies.

### Consultations

Learning Lab staff act as thought partners for your teaching and will help bring your creative or technology-driven ideas to life while connecting you and your students with the resources best positioned to meet your needs.

We start our process by talking with you about your course and goals, and learning about the **technologies and creative assignments** that you are interested in incorporating in your class. Faculty often also seek us out to help them think through how to use the physical space of their classroom and the time allotted, and how to deal with changes in enrollment or teaching staff.

### Assignment Design and Testing

Once we have a sense of your ideas and constraints, we can **develop prototype assignments** to send back to you or test with our **undergraduate user testers**. We can also review rubrics for creative assignments, generate prompts, or provide models for you to consider.

The Learning Lab offers a safe space to test new ideas about assignments and course activities. Testing represents the first stage at which course staff and faculty are able to see the ways in which a lesson plan or activity might actually unfold in real time. In addition to unforeseen infrastructural obstacles, one is able to get a sense of both the viability and the *value* of a hypothetical assignment or activity: what questions might be left unanswered, what unexpected answers might be available, and what ways the assignment could be better designed or made more focused, or creative, or effective.

### Workshops

If you are assigning students an activity or project that requires an unusual skill or tool, we can develop a workshop that supports students in their learning, **ensuring that students leave with the skills, tools, and attitudes required to successfully complete the assignment**. We can host your class in our studio space and use either part or all of your class's meeting time, or we can offer the workshop at another time.

Workshops are useful for teaching tools, possibilities, and skills, and also to open a space for the creative exploration of ideas, to generate new ways of thinking about the work, and to give the students the freedom to try new things—productively and playfully!

### Hackathons

For some courses or projects, students need **additional time and access to our staff, supplies, and resources**. In these cases, we are happy to offer "hackathons," time set aside for your students to work collaboratively in our space.

Hackathons typically occur just before an assignment is due. Our goal is to **give students a space to work that supports them**—in all ways—as they complete the assignment. If they need to shoot interviews or record audio of a performance, we will help them in our studio. If they need to edit video in Final Cut Pro X, design a 3D game in Unity, or polish off a podcast in Garageband, we will set up computers and provide staff and student experts in the relevant tools and disciplines.

One of the things we are fending off with the hackathon is the "unknown unknown" of the multimodal assignment. Students have a strong intuitive sense of how long a response paper or a 10-page term paper takes, based on experience. They do not have this sort of intuition for the podcast, for the infographic, for the edited on-camera performance. So with the hackathon we take what is an unknown (and for the student unknowable) task on their "to-do list" and we reduce it to a simple 2-3 hr appointment on their calendar, and we promise to support them—technically, pedagogically and, at times, emotionally—as they polish the piece off for their class.

### Symposia and Capstone events

As the Learning Lab studio is forever changeable and customizable, we work together with courses to adapt our space—that is, our tables, chairs, monitors, cameras, stage—to the needs of their specific live events. Early in the semester we meet with the course instructor to draft plans for the organization of the physical space, and to help the professor troubleshoot the shape of the event itself. Closer to the actual symposia, we also offer students the opportunity to rehearse their presentations in the LL studio so that they can practice using the tools that they'll have at the actual event.

At a 50-person capacity, we've had great success hosting students, professors, and stakeholders from the greater Boston community in our studio space. Past events have included:
- poster fairs
- musical performances
- artistic installations
- pop-up museum exhibits
- banks of digital presentation stations
- classic symposia featuring a single speaker
